Weights

In the final section, we will adjust the losing weight of our model to examine a good place for our bot(s). For this section, we pit our supercharged bot with 3 other normal bots using just the basic speed model from before. Due to the large amount of data used, where around 1 million mahjong games were played in this section, I only kept the aggregated data for each weight to save on space and computation power. Plots

From the above 4 charts, we can get the following findings:

  • Wins increase as the weight goes towards 0 from the negative numbers. Into the positive numbers, the wins decrease.
  • Losses slowly increase as we turn from the negatives to 0. Then there was a sharp increase at 0, followed by another slow but steady increase as we go into the positives.
  • Tsumos increase for lower negative weights. Going into the positives, we once again see a decrease.
  • Most importantly, the W/L ratio increases slowly. Then plateaus at the low 1.3s at -3 until -0.1. At 0, the W/L ratio is 1.25, a sharp decrease without the algorithm accounting for the dangerous tiles. After that, the W/L ratio decreases sharply as the algorithm starts to discard the most dangerous tiles, as expected.

To end things, a weight of -3 until -0.1 is generally sufficient to gaurantee an above 1.25 W/L ratio, which means that on average for every 4 direct losses, our player will win 5 times. If I was asked for one specific value, I would go for a weight of -2.5. This is the point at which the curve stay relatively flat while risking the least, noting that more negative weights deliver less losses, indicating more steady play.
